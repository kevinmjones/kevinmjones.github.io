01 · The Big Idea

Three desks, one strict boss

Think of FenixFire as a tiny trading firm with three desks. The most important rule of the firm: the clever desk never touches the money.

🔍

The Analyst suggests

Every morning it scans the market for stocks that are unusually busy, and watches the first half hour of trading. If a stock breaks above its early-morning range with conviction, the analyst writes up a suggestion: "buy this, here's my exit if I'm wrong, here's my target if I'm right." It can only suggest — nothing more.

⚖️

The Risk Boss decides

A completely separate, unbendable rulebook of 20 checks. Is the bet small enough? Is too much money already in play? Have we lost twice in a row today? Is it too close to closing time? One failed check = automatic no. The boss can't be sweet-talked — it isn't smart, it's strict, and that's the point. It is the only one allowed to approve an order.

🤝

The Clerk acts

Once a trade is approved, the clerk places it with a safety bracket already attached: a stop-loss order (sells automatically if it drops) and a profit target (sells automatically if it rises). The clerk babysits every order each minute and sells everything before the market closes — the robot never holds stocks overnight.

Why this design? Smart software can be wrong with great confidence. So the "smart" part is only allowed to make suggestions, and a dumb-but-strict rulebook holds the only key to the cash drawer.
02 · Data Flow

Where the information comes from, where the orders go

Market prices flow in from data providers and get saved locally, so the robot can also rehearse against past days. Orders flow out through one — and only one — guarded door.

every arrow is one-way · suggestions can only become orders by passing the risk boss

Everything that happens — every suggestion, every veto, every fill — is written into a permanent diary. If the program crashes and restarts, it reads the diary first, checks that its books match the broker's books, and refuses to trade if anything looks off.

03 · A Day in the Life

What a trading day looks like

Before 9:30 AM

Wake up & sanity check

Read the diary, reconcile the books, pick today's shortlist of unusually busy stocks. Yesterday's losing streak counter resets.

9:30 – 9:35 AM

Watch, don't touch

The market open is chaotic. The robot just observes the first five minutes to learn each stock's opening range.

9:35 – 11:00 AM

The hunting window

If a stock breaks out of its opening range on heavy volume, the analyst suggests a trade, the risk boss votes, and approved trades go in with their safety brackets. Most suggestions get vetoed — a quiet day is a normal day.

All day, every minute

Babysitting

The clerk checks every open position once a minute: did the stop trigger? the target? Is anything out of sync with the broker?

3:50 – 3:55 PM

Pack up, no exceptions

Anything still open gets sold. The robot always sleeps with empty pockets — no overnight surprises.

After close

Write the diary

Final reconciliation, profit/loss recorded, and the dashboard updates so Kevin can review the day.

04 · The Seatbelts

Five safety rules that can't be turned off by code

🧪

Practice money only

Real trading is hard-wired off. Turning it on requires Kevin to flip a switch, hold a valid login, and personally write a sign-off file no program is allowed to create.

🤏

Small bets, always

Each trade risks a fixed sliver of the account. Hard caps stop too much money being in play at once or piled into one stock. No borrowed money, ever.

🪢

Every trade wears a bracket

No position exists without an automatic exit below it (the stop) and above it (the target). Losses are pre-decided and small.

🔕

Silence means no

When human approval is on, ideas wait in a queue on Kevin's dashboard with a countdown. If nobody clicks approve in time, the answer is automatically no.

🛑

The big red switch

One kill switch cancels every order, sells everything, and halts trading. It stays tripped through restarts until a human investigates and types RESET.

05 · The Money Math

Why the dashboard says $5,205

The practice account started with a pretend $5,000. Over a month of rehearsed trading days, it made and lost small amounts that add up like a bank statement:

Pretend deposit (the start)$5,000.00
Won across 9 winning days — e.g. +$92.64, +$113.75…+ $329.14
Lost across losing days — each capped small by its bracket− $124.02
Account today$5,205.12

The “P&L today: −$9.38” tile only describes the most recent day (one small losing trade). The +$205.12 is the whole month. Both are true at once — like “I spent $9 today” and “I'm up $205 this month.”

Honest small print: 24 practice trades is far too few to prove the strategy works. The system itself stamps every report with a warning saying exactly that, and it refuses to consider real money until there's much more evidence — by design.
06 · Questions Parents Ask

The FAQ

So… is it using real money right now?
No. Every trade is simulated with practice money. The real-money pathway exists only as a locked door: the code's one "live" command refuses to run, full stop, even if every precondition is met.
Could the AI go rogue and bet everything?
It can't. The AI part can only write suggestions onto a list. The only component that can place an order is the rulebook-checker, which is ordinary, non-clever code that anyone can read — and it rejects anything oversized, late, unprotected, or repeated after losses.
What happens if the computer crashes mid-trade?
Everything important is written to disk the moment it happens. On restart, the robot reads its diary, compares its records with the broker's, adopts any orphaned positions (giving them a protective stop), and halts if the books don't match.
Is it making money?
In a month of practice it turned $5,000 into $5,205 — about +4%. Genuinely encouraging, genuinely meaningless at this sample size. The next step is months more practice plus testing against years of historical data.
What's with the name?
A phoenix rises from the ashes carefully and on a schedule — which is roughly the vibe: ambitious, but wearing five seatbelts.
