What Kevin’s Weather Computer Actually Does

A plain-English explanation, no jargon allowed.

The game it plays

There’s a website called Kalshi where people can bet small amounts on simple yes-or-no questions about real events. One of those questions, every single day, is:

“How hot will it get at Los Angeles International Airport today?”

The possible answers are split into ranges, like boxes you can put your money on:

70° or less18¢
71–72°20¢
73–74°25¢
75–76°22¢
77° or more15¢

Each box has a price between 1¢ and 99¢. If you buy a box for 25¢ and the temperature lands in your box, you get $1. If it doesn’t, you lose your 25¢. The prices move all day as people change their minds — just like a little stock market, except the “stock” is tomorrow’s thermometer reading.

At the end of the day, the National Weather Service publishes the official high temperature for the airport. That official number — nobody else’s — decides who was right.

How a 25¢ box becomes a dollar

So what did Kevin build?

A computer program that tries to predict that official number better than the crowd does, and spots moments when the crowd’s prices look wrong. It works around the clock in four steps:

  1. It watches the weather, constantly. Every few minutes it collects the actual readings from the airport’s weather station — temperature, clouds, wind — plus the official government forecasts. It has also memorized over two years of LA weather history.
  2. It makes its own forecast. Not just one number, but odds for every box: “30% chance of 73–74°, 20% chance of 75–76°…” LA is tricky because of the morning fog bank off the ocean (the famous “June Gloom”) — whether it burns off early or lingers can swing the day’s high by several degrees, so the program pays special attention to the clouds before lunch.
  3. It compares its odds to the prices. If the program thinks a box has a 40% chance of winning but people are selling it for 25¢, that’s a bargain — like finding a $40 item priced at $25. If there are no bargains, it does nothing. Most of the time, doing nothing is the right move, and it knows that.
  4. It grades its own homework. Every evening it checks the official number, scores how good its forecast was, writes itself a report card, and adjusts. When it’s wrong, it studies why it was wrong and fixes that specific weakness.

The whole machine on one napkin

Why the fog matters so much

Same city, same season — six degrees apart, decided almost entirely by whether the ocean fog burns off before lunch. That one question moves more money on this market than anything else, which is why the program stares at the morning cloud reports the way a farmer stares at the sky.

A real example from this week

One clear morning, the program predicted a cooler day than everyone else — its favorite box was 71–72°, while the crowd was betting on 75–76°. The temperature ended up in the middle. Digging into it, Kevin found two real problems: the program’s weather readings were arriving 40 minutes late, and on fog-free mornings it consistently guessed a degree too cool because it leaned too hard on the recent cool stretch of days.

The same day, both got fixed: it now gets readings within minutes, and it learned to use the government’s big “blend of all forecasts” as its starting point on days that break the pattern. That fast find-the-flaw-and-fix-it loop is the project.

What a “bargain” looks like

Most boxes, most days, are priced about right — the orange and blue bars match, and the program passes. It only acts when its bar towers over the price. Some days that never happens, and a day with zero bets is counted as a good day, not a boring one.

The part that matters: it’s play money

No real money is at risk. The system trades with pretend dollars — what traders call “paper trading.” It keeps an honest ledger of every imaginary bet, win or lose, exactly as if it were real. The program is literally built so that it cannot place a real-money trade: that mode doesn’t exist in the code, on purpose.

The idea is simple: prove it works on paper first, with months of honest records. Only with that proof in hand would real money — in small, strictly limited amounts — ever be considered.

Questions you might be wondering

Is this gambling?
It’s closer to insurance or farming decisions than a casino. In a casino the odds are fixed against you; here, someone who genuinely predicts weather better than the crowd has a real edge. Kalshi is a legal, U.S.-government-regulated exchange. And again — right now it’s all pretend money.
Couldn’t you just read the weather forecast like everyone else?
Everyone does — that’s the point. To beat the crowd you need something extra: faster data, better math on the fog, and the discipline to only bet when the numbers genuinely disagree with the price. Computers are very good at that kind of patience.
What does Kevin actually do all day, then?
He’s the mechanic, not the driver. The system runs itself; Kevin studies its report cards, finds its blind spots, and upgrades it — often by directing a team of AI assistants that test improvements against years of history before anything goes live.
What if it’s wrong?
It’s wrong a lot — every forecaster is. The goal isn’t to be right every day; it’s to be slightly less wrong than the crowd, over hundreds of days. The system also has safety rules: if its data feeds go quiet or its confidence is low, it refuses to bet at all.
